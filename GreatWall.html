The Great Wall of China

Two Thousand-Year History

The Great Wall of China is a series of fortifications made of stone, brick, tamped earth, wood and other materials, generally built from east to west across the historical northern borders of China, primarily to protect the Chinese Empire or its prototypical states against intrusions by various nomadic groups or military incursions by warlike peoples or forces. Several walls were built as early as the 7th century BC. These were later joined together and are now collectively referred to as the Great Wall.

Map of Great Wall

The main Great Wall line stretches from Shanhaiguan in the east to Lop Lake in the west along an arc that roughly delineates the southern edge of Inner Mongolia. The walls built during the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644) measure 5,500 miles. They are made up of 3,889 miles of sections of actual wall, 223 miles of trenches and 1,387 miles of natural defensive barriers such as hills and rivers. The entire Great Wall with all its branches measures out to 13,171 miles.

Great Wall in Winter

Other purposes of the Great Wall have included border controls for the imposition of duties on goods transported along the Silk Road, regulation or encouragement of trade, and the control of immigration and emigration. The defensive characteristics of the Great Wall were enhanced by the construction of watch towers, troop barracks and garrison stations with signaling capabilities through the means of smoke or fire. Finally, the the Great Wall also served as a transportation corridor.
